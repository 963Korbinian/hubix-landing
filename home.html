Under the hood Architecture · pre-launch · Pilot Q4 2026 · Galle

How HUBIX makes an honest hour of driving pay.

A neighbour already driving across town could carry your parcel. The problem was never the driver — it's the math. One parcel per trip earns a driver about LKR 60 an hour, less than the petrol; stack the right parcels onto the same road and that hour could clear LKR 150 or more. These are illustrative unit economics — the pilot exists to prove them. Finding those stacks, every minute, for every driver, across a whole city, is a routing problem no human can solve by hand. That's the one job our AI is built to do.

01
The Bundle Engine

Parcels that share a road, found automatically.

Every order is a point on a map — a pickup, a drop-off, a size, a deadline. Our optimizer, built on Google OR-Tools and PyVRP (the same class of solver that routes commercial fleets), continuously searches for the parcels that happen to share a road. It doesn't hand a driver a growing to-do list — it hands them one finished, fully-sequenced route. The driver takes the whole bundle as-is, or leaves it for the next one. We call it Pick-a-whole-Bundle — the plan is optimized before it ever reaches a phone.

Google OR-ToolsPyVRPVRP solver
02
The Continuous Order Book

The plan never freezes. It re-solves every 60 seconds.

Real demand doesn't arrive in tidy batches, so we never freeze the plan. Every 60 seconds the order book re-solves from scratch against whatever is on the map right now. Anything that keeps getting passed over earns an automatic price boost the longer it waits — until it becomes worth someone's short detour. Nothing sits forgotten in a corner. It's a marketplace built to clear itself, minute by minute.

60s re-solveaging price-boost
03
The Self-Learning Loop

The road overwrites the map.

Google Maps is optimistic about Sri Lankan roads — a tuk-tuk stuck behind a bus on the Galle Road doesn't move at map speed. So every estimate starts with a ×1.35 reality correction, and then the road overwrites it: once a lane has enough real trips behind it (~30), the system trusts its own measured times over the map's. It also treats a bus or a train as a valid leg. Every handover makes the next prediction sharper — a compounding advantage that grows with the network.

04
The Trust Layer

Clever routing is worthless if the chain isn't trusted.

A parcel changes hands up to five times, and every single handover is a two-sided handshake backed by a photo — logged, timestamped, and attached to that parcel's history. Senders, hubs, connectors and recipients each carry their own trust score. The trust history a network like this accumulates — who did what, reliably, over time — is the real moat: the one thing a competitor can't copy over a weekend.

None of this is magic. It's a routing engine, a continuous order book, and a learning loop — all pointed at one question: how do we make an honest hour of driving genuinely pay? Answer that, and the network starts to build itself.

The difference

Why this isn't just
another courier.

Traditional couriers move parcels by owning everything — the vans, the riders, the routes. That works. It's also why the economics only add up at high volume, on corridors big enough to justify a truck. The everyday shipments — a small shop sending five orders down one road — are exactly what falls through those gaps. HUBIX starts from the other end. We don't buy vehicles. Shops become hubs. Neighbours already making the trip become connectors. And by design, an AI dispatcher bundles loose orders into one optimized run — recomputed every 60 seconds — so a single ride can carry many parcels instead of one.

01 · The market

Fragmented, thin outside Colombo

Sri Lanka's domestic parcel flow is fragmented, cash-on-delivery-heavy, and thin everywhere outside the Colombo corridor. Shops move goods through informal favours, buses, and three-wheelers — reliable enough to survive, too unreliable to scale. We start where the pain is sharpest and the incumbents are weakest: Galle and the Southern Province.

02 · Why now

The rails are missing, not the demand

Smartphones and shop-to-consumer commerce have reached the south, but no one has built a trusted delivery network beneath them. Whoever earns trust on these corridors first sets the standard — and that window is open now, not forever.

03 · The moat

Trust-takes-all, not winner-takes-all

P2P logistics isn't winner-takes-all — it's trust-takes-all. Every handoff we verify (two-sided handshake plus photo trail across all five touchpoints) compounds into a delivery history that's hard to buy or backfill. AI is the catalyst, not the moat — it makes bundling economical; the network and its trust record are what's hard to copy. We estimate a 24–36 month first-mover window to build that lead.

04 · The architecture

Asset-light, self-optimising by design

Shops become hubs, neighbours become connectors, no fleet on our books. A continuous order book re-optimises every 60 seconds; an OR-Tools + PyVRP solver bundles single parcels into multi-order routes a driver takes as one whole. Built to learn real Sri Lankan travel times against prediction — from a ×1.35 correction, refined once enough trips accrue (~30 per lane) — and treats buses and trains as first-class transport modes.

The economic thesis. A driver carrying single parcels earns too little to show up twice. Bundled into optimised multi-order stacks, the same hour becomes worth doing. That gap — from unviable to viable — is the entire reason the routing engine exists. It's the problem we engineer around, stated as a model, not a claim of traction.
